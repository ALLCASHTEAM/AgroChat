Pepper is a horticultural crop that is grown for its edible fruits, which are used as spices, vegetables, or condiments. Pepper is also a cash crop in some regions, where the fruits are sold fresh, dried, or processed into various products1. Pepper leaf blight is a fungal disease caused by Phytophthora capsici, which belongs to the order Peronosporales (oomycetes). It infects the leaves, stems, roots, and fruits of pepper plants, causing necrotic lesions, wilting, and rotting1 2.
The mode of reproduction of the pathogenic fungus of this disease is mainly by sporangia, which are sac-like structures that produce zoospores. The zoospores are motile spores that can swim in water and infect new host plants. The fungus can also produce oospores, which are thick-walled sexual spores that can survive in the soil or on plant debris for long periods2. The epidemiological pattern and mode of transmission of this disease are influenced by several factors, such as host susceptibility, environmental conditions, and inoculum sources. Pepper leaf blight is more severe on susceptible varieties of pepper, especially those with large leaves and dense canopies. The disease is favored by warm (2030Г‚В°C) and wet (high rainfall or irrigation) weather, which promotes spore production and infection. The inoculum sources include infected plant debris, volunteer plants, alternative hosts (such as tomato, eggplant, squash), and soil-borne or water-borne spores from nearby or distant fields2 3.
The period of disease onset for this disease depends on the availability of inoculum and favorable weather conditions. Pepper leaf blight can appear on any part of the pepper plant above or below ground and spreads rapidly throughout the plant. The disease can reduce fruit quality and yield by up to 100%2. The environmental conditions required for the disease are warm (20-30Г‚В°C) and wet (high rainfall or irrigation) weather, which promotes spore production and infection. The disease is also more severe in poorly drained soils, high soil pH (\&gt;7), high soil organic matter (\&gt;4%), and high soil fertility2 3.
The overwintering method of this disease is by oospores, which are thick-walled sexual spores that can survive in the soil or on plant debris for long periods. The oospores can germinate and produce sporangia when favorable conditions occur2.
According to 1, fungicides such as chlorothalonil, mancozeb and copper can be used to control fungal diseases of pepper, including leaf blight. The type of agent, application dose, application period, application frequency and control effect may vary depending on the product label, disease severity and environmental conditions. Always follow the label instructions and safety precautions when using fungicides.
According to 1 and 2, infected plant parts and debris should be removed and destroyed to reduce the inoculum of fungal pathogens. Crop rotation with non-host plants can also help to prevent disease buildup in the soil. Avoid overhead watering and splashing soil onto the leaves when watering.
According to 1, some antagonistic microorganisms such as Bacillus subtilis, Pseudomonas fluorescens and Trichoderma harzianum have been reported to suppress fungal diseases of pepper by producing antibiotics, enzymes or inducing plant resistance. These biocontrol agents can be applied as seed treatments, soil amendments or foliar sprays.
According to 1 and 2, some cultural practices that can reduce the incidence and severity of fungal diseases of pepper are: planting resistant or tolerant varieties, avoiding excessive irrigation and fertilizer application, maintaining optimal plant spacing and weed control, avoiding mechanical injury to plants and fruits, and harvesting fruits as soon as they mature.

According to 2 and 3, a quick test method for the phytopathogenic fungus of pepper leaf blight disease is the immunostrip test, which is a lateral flow immunoassay that detects specific antigens of Phytophthora capsici in plant samples. The immunostrip test is simple, rapid, and reliable, and can be performed in the field or in the laboratory. The test requires a small piece of infected plant tissue, a buffer solution, and an immunostrip device. The plant tissue is macerated in the buffer solution and then applied to the immunostrip device. If Phytophthora capsici is present in the sample, a purple line will appear on the immunostrip device within 10 minutes.
