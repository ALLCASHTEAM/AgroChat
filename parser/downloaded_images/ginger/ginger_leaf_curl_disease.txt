Ginger is a horticultural crop that is grown for its aromatic rhizomes1. Ginger leaf curl disease is a bacterial disease caused by Ralstonia solanacearum, also known as ginger blast or bacterial wilt1 2.
The pathogenic bacterium of ginger leaf curl disease does not have a fungal mode of reproduction, but rather reproduces by binary fission2. The epidemiological pattern and mode of transmission of ginger leaf curl disease are soilborne and water-borne. The bacterium can survive in the soil for long periods and infect ginger plants through wounds or natural openings. It can also spread through irrigation water, runoff water, or contaminated tools1 2.
The period of disease onset for ginger leaf curl disease varies depending on the environmental conditions and the stage of plant growth. The disease can appear as early as 5 to 7 days after infection, or as late as 3 to 4 months after planting1 2. The environmental conditions required for the disease are high temperature, high humidity, and poor drainage. The disease is more severe in poorly drained soils that favor waterlogging and anaerobic conditions1 2.
The overwintering method of this disease is through survival in the soil or in infected plant debris. The bacterium can persist in the soil for up to 2 years, or longer if there are alternate hosts or organic matter present. It can also overwinter in infected rhizomes or stems that are left in the field or stored improperly1 2.
Applying copper-based fungicides or antibiotics such as streptomycin or tetracycline can help reduce the bacterial population and prevent further spread of the disease. However, these chemicals have limited efficacy and may pose environmental and health risks1.
Cutting off and destroying the infected parts of the plant can help prevent the disease from spreading to healthy plants. However, this method is labor-intensive and may not be feasible for large-scale cultivation1.
Using antagonistic microorganisms such as <em>Pseudomonas fluorescens</em>, <em>Bacillus subtilis</em>, or <em>Trichoderma harzianum</em> can help suppress the growth and activity of the pathogen. These biocontrol agents can be applied as seed treatments, soil amendments, or foliar sprays1.
Practicing crop rotation, intercropping, mulching, weed control, and proper drainage can help reduce the incidence and severity of the disease. Avoiding planting ginger in fields with a history of bacterial wilt or using disease-free planting materials can also help prevent the disease1.
